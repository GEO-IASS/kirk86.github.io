The fundamental theorem of arithmetic in number theory states that every positive integer number greater than 1, x ∈ Z where x > 1, is a prime number or it is comprised of the unique product of prime numbers up to the order of the factors.

Example:

1250 = 21 × 54 = 2× 5× 5× 5× 5
1200 = 31× 52× 24 = 3× 5× 5× 2× 2× 2× 2

In other words:

  1. 1250 and 1200 can be represented as a product of primes
  2. there will always be a specific amount of primes in each product

Proof:

Using Euclid's lemma and B\`{e}zout's identity. Let p be a prime. Assume p divides the product of two integers a and b written as (p|ab), and its negation is ¬ (p|ab).

Then either p divides a (p|a) or p divides b (p|b) or both. Some equivalent statements:

  • if ¬ (p|a) and ¬ (p|b), then ¬ (p|ab).
  • if ¬ (p|a) and (p|ab), then (p|b).

Generalization of Euclid's lemma from prime numbers to any integers:

  • if (n|ab), and n shares no common divisors other than 1 with a, then (n|b).

If n is prime, either:

  • (n|a) or
  • n shares no common divisors other than 1 (i.e. relatively prime) with a, therefore, ¬ (n|a) so (n|b).

Thus, this is a generalization.

B\'{e}zout's identity states that if x and y are share no common divisor other than 1 (i.e. are relatively prime integers) there exist integers r and s such that:

rx + sy = 1.

Let a and b share no common divisor other than 1, and assume that (n|ab). According to B\'{e}zout's identity there exist r and s which provide:

rn + sa = 1.

Multiplying both sides by b:

rnb + sab = b.

rnb is divisible by n and sab is divisible by ab which in turn is also divisible by n as we state above in our assumption.

Therefore, since we have shown that both terms in the addition of rnb + sab are divisible by n then it must be true that their sum b, is also divisible by n.

This is also a generalization to Euclid's lemma mentioned above.
